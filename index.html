Navigating Animal Destinies in Austin: An Analysis of Shelter Intake and Outcomes

Austin Animal Center faces the challenge of handling a large number of animals each year. Over the past decade, the average total number of animals admitted per year has reached 13,270 (complete years only). While many of these animals successfully transition to adoptive homes or reunite with their owners, a significant number face outcomes such as transfer to rescue partners or unfortunate euthanasia. This page will analyze Austin Animal Center's internal admissions from 2013–2025 and reveal possible outcome patterns. Our goal is to uncover key influencing factors and identify data-driven strategies to improve lifesaving efforts for vulnerable animals in the Austin area.

Key Findings from Intake Data: Animal Types and Health Conditions

Our initial analysis of the intake records at the Austin Animal Center provides crucial insights into the population it serves. The distribution of animal types (as shown in the pie chart below) clearly indicates that dogs (54.5%) and cats (39.8%) are the predominant species, collectively accounting for over 94% of all intakes. Other categories such as 'Other' animals (5.14%), birds (0.501%), and livestock (a very small 0.0191%) represent a much smaller fraction of the shelter's incoming population, suggesting that resource allocation for housing and species-specific care should primarily focus on canines and felines.

Turning to the health conditions upon arrival (visualized in the bar chart below with a log scale), the data overwhelmingly shows that the majority of animals enter the shelter in 'Normal' health, with this category significantly outnumbering all others. Following 'Normal,' 'Injured' and 'Sick' animals constitute the next most substantial groups, indicating a consistent need for veterinary assessment and treatment resources. While conditions like 'Nursing,' 'Neonatal,' 'Medical,' and 'Aged' are also notably present, other more specific or acute conditions such as 'Parvo,' 'Agonal,' or 'Panleuk' appear in much smaller numbers, though their presence still necessitates specialized care protocols. This initial assessment underscores the diverse needs of incoming animals, from routine care for generally healthy individuals to immediate and intensive medical support for those arriving injured or ill.

How Intake Condition Shapes Animal Outcomes

The initial state of an animal upon arrival at the Austin Animal Center plays a pivotal role in determining its subsequent journey. The interactive chart below visualizes the distribution of outcomes (like Adoption, Transfer, Euthanasia, etc.) for each distinct intake condition.

Observe how animals entering in 'Normal' health predominantly find positive outcomes, primarily through 'Adoption'. Conversely, as intake conditions become more challenging—such as 'Injured', 'Sick', 'Feral', or 'Neonatal'—the spectrum of outcomes shifts. While 'Adoption' and 'Transfer' to rescue partners remain crucial life-saving avenues, the rates of 'Euthanasia' or 'Died' unfortunately increase for these more vulnerable populations. Conditions like 'Parvo' or 'Agonal' starkly illustrate the severe prognoses some animals face.
